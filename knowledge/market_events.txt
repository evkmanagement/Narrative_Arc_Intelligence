Inflation Reduction Act overhauls federal EV tax credit and adds North America final assembly requirement On August 16, 2022, the Inflation Reduction Act was signed into law, renaming the existing plug‑in vehicle credit as the “clean vehicle credit,” extending it through 2032, eliminating the 200,000‑vehicle manufacturer cap, and introducing new income and MSRP caps and battery/critical mineral sourcing requirements that phase in starting in 2023. Federal new and used clean vehicle tax credits expire for vehicles purchased after September 30, 2025 Following enactment of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, federal clean vehicle credits worth up to $7,500 for new EVs and $4,000 for used EVs ceased to be available for vehicles purchased or leased after September 30, 2025, with eligibility limited to buyers who had a binding contract and payment in place by that date. Consumer and tax guidance clarified that purchases completed after the cutoff would no longer qualify for the Section 30D new clean vehicle credit or the used EV credit despite earlier expectations of availability through 2032 under the Inflation Reduction Act.

[YEAR:2026] [ID:SIG-ECO-2025-016] [CAT:Economy]
U.S. EV sales surge ahead of September 30, 2025 tax credit cutoff NPR reported that EV sales in the United States surged in the months leading up to the September 30, 2025 expiration of federal EV tax credits, as buyers rushed to secure vehicles or sign binding purchase contracts before incentives ended. Analysis from CleanTechnica described this pre‑deadline period as a major sales “pull‑forward,” reflecting consumers accelerating purchases to capture the up‑to‑$7,500 new EV credit before it was eliminated under the One Big Beautiful Bill Act.

[YEAR:2026] [ID:SIG-ECO-2025-017] [CAT:Economy]
U.S. EV sales dip about 2% year-over-year in 2025 after Q4 collapse, with EV share at roughly 7.8% Cox Automotive estimates reported by Kelley Blue Book and other outlets indicated that U.S. EV sales totaled about 1.28 million units in 2025, down roughly 2% from an estimated 1.30 million in 2024, marking the first annual decline after several years of growth. EVs accounted for about 7.8% of 2025 new‑vehicle sales versus 8.1% in 2024, with EV share peaking around 10.5% in Q3 2025 before dropping to about 5.8% in Q4 following the September 30 end of the federal EV tax credits.

[YEAR:2026] [ID:SIG-ECO-2025-018] [CAT:Economy]
EV market share plateaus near 8.6% through first five months of 2025 despite rising EV registrations S&P Global Mobility reported that from January through May 2025, new EV retail registrations in the United States rose about 8.1% year‑over‑year to more than 450,000 units, but EV market share remained largely flat at around 8.6%, only 0.2 percentage points higher than a year earlier. The analysis noted that April and May 2025 were the first months since 2020 in which EV market share declined compared with the same months the prior year, indicating that EV sales growth was no longer keeping pace with overall vehicle demand.

[YEAR:2026] [ID:SIG-ECO-2025-019] [CAT:Economy]
Tesla U.S. EV market share declines to 46% in 2025 as GM and other brands gain share CleanTechnica’s analysis of 2025 U.S. EV sales found that Tesla’s share of the U.S. EV market declined from about 48.7% in 2024 to about 46% in 2025, with Tesla’s U.S. EV sales falling from roughly 634,000 to about 589,000 units. Over the same period, General Motors’ EV market share increased from about 8.8% to 13.2%, with GM’s EV sales rising from around 92,000 to more than 150,000 units, while several other automakers such as Hyundai–Kia and Volkswagen Group also saw shifts in EV market share.
